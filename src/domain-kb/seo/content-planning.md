## Topic Cluster Model

A topic cluster is a pillar page (broad, comprehensive) linked bidirectionally to a set of supporting articles (narrow, deep). The structure signals topical authority to Google's crawlers better than isolated articles.

```
Pillar: "Complete Guide to Email Marketing"
├── Supporting: "Email Subject Line Best Practices"
├── Supporting: "How to Segment an Email List"
├── Supporting: "Email Deliverability Troubleshooting"
└── Supporting: "A/B Testing Email Campaigns"
```

Every supporting article links back to the pillar. The pillar links to every supporting article. No orphan pages.

## When to Use a Cluster vs. a Single Article

| Situation | Approach |
|---|---|
| Broad topic with many subtopics | Pillar + cluster |
| Narrow question with one clear answer | Single article |
| Topic where competitors have 20+ pages | Build a full cluster to compete |
| Niche query with low search demand | Single article; don't over-produce |

Do not build a cluster because it feels thorough. Build one because the topic warrants it and there is search demand across multiple subtopics.

## Pillar Page Requirements

- Covers the topic at high altitude: definitions, key concepts, major subtopics
- Does not fully exhaust any one subtopic — that's what supporting articles do
- Targets a broad head keyword (high volume, competitive)
- Long enough to be comprehensive (often 2,000–4,000 words) but not padded

## Supporting Article Requirements

- Targets a specific long-tail variant or subtopic
- Goes deep on one angle the pillar summarizes
- Has a unique search intent vs. every other article in the cluster
- Contains an internal link back to the pillar with relevant anchor text

## Content Calendar Planning

Plan in 30-day sprints. Establish the pillar first; the cluster depends on it for internal linking.

```
Week 1: Publish pillar page
Week 2: Supporting article 1 + backlink to pillar
Week 3: Supporting article 2 + backlink to pillar
Week 4: Supporting article 3 + backlink to pillar
```

Don't publish all supporting articles before the pillar exists. The internal linking structure should be complete at publication, not retrofitted.

## When NOT to Plan a Cluster

- No existing domain authority: one strong standalone article will rank faster than a thin cluster
- The topic has fewer than 3–4 viable supporting subtopics with independent search demand
- You don't have the resources to produce the cluster within 60 days — a half-built cluster is worse than a standalone article

## Common Failure Modes

- **Pillar without depth**: Pillar pages that just list subtopics without useful content don't anchor the cluster. The pillar must stand alone as a resource.
- **Supporting articles with no clear differentiation**: If two supporting articles answer the same question from the same angle, you've created cannibalization within your own cluster.
- **Missing internal links**: Publishing cluster articles without linking them to the pillar defeats the purpose. Internal linking is structural, not optional.
- **Planning for keywords, not intent**: Two keywords with the same intent belong in one article, not two separate cluster nodes.
