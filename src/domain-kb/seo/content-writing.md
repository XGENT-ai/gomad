# SEO Content Writing

## Structure First

Write the outline before writing a word of content. Each H2 maps to a searcher question or subtopic. If you can't define what each section answers before you write it, the content will meander.

Recommended structure:
1. **Introduction** (50–100 words): Hook + what the reader will learn + primary keyword in first 100 words
2. **Body**: 3–6 H2 sections, each with 2–3 H3 subsections if needed
3. **FAQ** (optional): Captures PAA (People Also Ask) queries
4. **No explicit conclusion**: End on the last substantive section — summaries are filler

## Keyword Integration

- Primary keyword: 0.5–1.5% density. At 300 words that's 1–4 mentions; at 1,500 words it's 7–22 mentions.
- Use the keyword in: H1, first 100 words, at least one H2, meta title
- Use semantic variations (LSI terms) throughout — not repetitions of the exact keyword
- Never stuff. "The best email marketing software is the best email marketing software for…" fails both humans and crawlers

## E-E-A-T Signals to Include Inline

- Specific data with year and source: "According to HubSpot's 2025 State of Marketing report, 78% of..."
- First-person experience where applicable: "When we tested this with a 10,000-subscriber list..."
- Expert quotes with named attribution and credentials
- Practical, specific advice — not "it depends" generalities

## Readability Standards

| Metric | Target |
|---|---|
| Paragraph length | 2–3 sentences max |
| Sentence length | Under 20 words on average |
| Reading level | Grade 8–10 (use Hemingway App to check) |
| Header frequency | H2 every 300–400 words |
| Bullet/numbered lists | For 3+ parallel items; not for prose |

## Introduction Pattern

```
[Hook — surprising stat, question, or scenario]
[Problem statement — what the reader is struggling with]
[Promise — what this article delivers]
[Primary keyword naturally embedded]
```

Bad introduction: "In this article, we will explore email marketing best practices. Email marketing is important for businesses."

Good introduction: "Email marketing returns $36 for every $1 spent — but only when the fundamentals are right. Most campaigns underperform not because of bad design, but because of fixable structural mistakes. Here's what separates high-performing campaigns from ignored ones."

## When NOT to Optimize for SEO

- Internal documentation: No search traffic, so keyword optimization adds noise
- Press releases: Different distribution model; SEO signals aren't the lever
- Short-form social content adapted to blog format: The length and structure don't support SEO content patterns

## Common Failure Modes

- **Writing for the keyword, not the reader**: Forcing a keyword into every paragraph creates unnatural phrasing that harms both readability and ranking.
- **Padding to hit a word count**: Google's quality raters penalize thin content that's been stretched. A focused 800-word article beats a padded 2,500-word one.
- **No clear answer in the introduction**: If the reader has to scroll 400 words to get the core answer, bounce rate increases and dwell time drops — both negative signals.
- **Generic advice**: "Use good SEO practices" is not actionable. "Use the primary keyword in the first 100 words and one H2" is.
